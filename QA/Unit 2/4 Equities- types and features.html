Question 1

[1013217] A fund made up of shares, bonds and property has achieved the below returns over one year. Assuming that the return from each type of security is paid at the end of the year, what is the annual rate of return for this fund?
A
3.65%
B
6.5%
C
8.45%
D
9.75%
The correct answer is: D - 9.75%

Explanation

The total return of the fund is the capital gain plus income divided by the start value of the fund. The period given is for one year, so the total return is also the annual return. (0.5 + 0.01 + 0.1 + 0.125 + 0.180 + 0.06) / (5 + 3 + 2) = 9.75%

Question 5

[1001095] When directors fail to declare a preference dividend, this sometimes confers the right of the preference shareholders to:
A
Wind-up the company
B
Vote at the AGM
C
Appoint a receiver
D
Convert their shares into ordinary shares
The correct answer is: B - Vote at the AGM

Explanation

Usually preference shares carry the right of cumulative dividends. This means that the right to any dividend not declared is rolled forward to the next period. The articles of association usually state that, should the directors fail to declare a dividend over a specified period of time (usually three years), the right to vote in company meetings is conferred on the preference shareholders.

Question 14

[1013297] A warrant issued by a company is:
A
A put option issued by a company in its own shares
B
A call option issued by a company in its own shares
C
An undertaking by a company to purchase its own shares at a premium price
D
An undertaking by a company to sell its own shares at a discount price
The correct answer is: B - A call option issued by a company in its own shares

Explanation

A warrant is the right to buy BRAND NEW shares in the issuing company. 'An undertaking by a company to sell its own shares at a discount price' is wrong because sometimes warrants expire 'out-of-the-money', which means that the price at which the warrant holder may buy the shares is higher than their prevailing market value. 'A call option issued by a company in its own shares' is the correct answer.

Question 15

[1001102] Which of the following is true of dividends in relation to ADR holders?
A
They are paid directly from the company to the holder
B
They are paid by the company to the depository bank
C
They are paid by the depository bank to the company
D
ADR holders do not receive dividends
The correct answer is: B - They are paid by the company to the depository bank

Explanation

ADR holders receive all privileges of the underlying shares, including voting rights, and dividends are paid in dollars. Dividends are received by the bank which holds the underlying shares (the depository bank), converted into dollars and paid to the holders. Note: ADR holders do not generally receive pre-emptive rights in rights issues.

Question 17

[1001110] An investor receives a dividend of £2.60 per share on a holding of 350 shares. For what amount is the tax credit? You should answer this question by typing in the following format without symbols: 000.00.
The correct answer is: 101.11 - 101.11

Explanation

First calculate the gross dividend: (£2.6/90) x 100 = £2.88889 Subtract the net dividend from the gross dividend to get the tax credit: £2.88889 - £2.60 = £0.28889 tax credit per share. Therefore total tax credit = 350 shares x £0.28889 = £101.11

Question 19

[1001098] Which of the following is true about preference shares?
A
They always carry the right to vote
B
The dividend can be waived
C
They are paid before debentures in the event of a liquidation
D
They only receive payment if ordinary share dividends have been made
The correct answer is: B - The dividend can be waived

Explanation

Preference shares pay a fixed rate of dividend each year based on the nominal value of the share. The dividend on preference shares can be waived by the board of directors but cannot be varied. However, if preference share dividends are not paid in a particular year, then ordinary shareholders cannot be paid dividends either. There are various types of preference shares.

Question 20

[1001094] A participating preference share:
A
Entitles the holder to additional dividends
B
Allows the holder to appoint a receiver should the company not pay a dividend
C
Entitles the holder to receive any arrears in dividends should they not be paid at the end of any one year
D
Allows the holder the right to convert into a predetermined number of ordinary shares
The correct answer is: A - Entitles the holder to additional dividends

Explanation

Shareholders, whether ordinary or preference, never have the right to appoint a receiver. The answer 'entitles the holder to receive any arrears in dividends should they not be paid at the end of any one year' describes a cumulative preference share, not a participating one. Allows the holder the right to convert into a predetermined number of ordinary shares' describes convertible preference shares. 'Entitles the holder to additional dividends at the discretion of the directors' is the correct answer.

Question 21

What is the formula value? You should answer this question by typing the correct numbers in pence in the following format without symbols: 0
The correct answer is: 0 - 0

Explanation

The formula value of a warrant is its intrinsic value. If the warrant is the right to buy at 440p and the shares are currently at 400p, the warrant has no built-in value. The formula value is zero.

Question 22

[1001107] Which of the following is NOT true of preference shares?
A
In case of liquidation, holders of preference shares have priority in terms of the payment of nominal value over ordinary shareholders
B
In case of liquidation, holders of preference shares have priority in terms of the payment of dividend arrears over ordinary shareholders
C
Preference shares usually pay dividends fixed as a percentage of the nominal value
D
If cumulative, dividend arrears must be paid before an ordinary dividend can be paid
The correct answer is: B - In case of liquidation, holders of preference shares have priority in terms of the payment of dividend arrears over ordinary shareholders

Explanation

In liquidation, preference shareholders have priority over ordinary shareholders in the repayment of the nominal value. Unless the articles specify otherwise, preference dividends will not usually be paid in liquidation.
