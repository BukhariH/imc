Question 2

[1015333] An investor buys some shares in company A for £15,000 and company B for £8,000. Some time later the shares in company A are sold for £13,000 and the shares in company B for £27,000. Assume that the investor pays 18% tax on capital gains and that the exemption is £11,000. What is the capital gains tax paid by the investor? You should answer this question by typing the correct numbers in the following format without symbols: 0,000
The correct answer is: 1,080 - 1,080

Explanation

The capital losses on share A can be offset against the capital gains on share B which gives a net gain of £17,000. The annual exemption can be deducted from this leaving a capital gain of £6,000. This is taxed at 18% and will give a tax bill of £6,000 x 18% = £1,080.

Explanation

Question 14

[1017643] Marie Sanchez has taxable earned income of £30,000 (after taking into account her personal allowance). She has realised a chargeable gain of £4,000 after using her CGT allowance. How much CGT is she due on this gain? You should answer this question by typing the correct numbers in the following format without symbols: 000.00
The correct answer is: 933.50 - 933.50

Explanation

The basic rate band ends at £31,865. Marie's gain moves beyond this band. For this reason she will be taxed on the gain partly at the basic rate (18%) and partly at the higher rate (28%). The first £1,865 of the gain is taxed at 18%: £1,865 x 0.18 = £335.70 The remaining £2,135 is taxed at 28%: £2,135 x 0.28 = £597.80 The tax due on the gain is: £335.70 + £597.80 = £933.50

Question 24

[1049946] Marcus is a higher rate taxpayer who wishes to invest in a well-diversified fund and gain some tax efficiency, but wants to take control of the timings of his purchases and sales. He has an internet share-dealing account with his retail bank. Which of the following would be the best investment?
A
He should invest in an authorised unit trust held in an ISA account
B
He should invest in an investment trust company held in an ISA account
C
He should invest in a unit-linked investment bond
D
He should invest in an EIS-qualifying company via his share-dealing account
The correct answer is: B - He should invest in an investment trust company held in an ISA account

Explanation

Marcus wants to gain tax efficiency, and all of the investments provide a certain tax benefit. All gains and income within an ISA account are tax-free, although tax credits on dividends cannot be reclaimed. Investment bonds allow a deferral of tax until encashment, when Marcus's tax liability may be lower. Enterprise investment schemes offer income and capital gain tax relief. However, Marcus also wanted a diversified fund: this eliminates the EIS-qualifying company. He also wanted control over his purchases and sales. This definitely eliminates the unit-linked bond, which is a fixed-term investment with early encashment penalties, and, to a lesser extent the unit trust, which is likely to have dilution levies charged on purchases and sales. The unit trust will also have less clarity on the pricing of purchases and sales, because of the, typically, forward-pricing. Investment trusts, as they trade on the secondary markets, have a more immediate pricing available and can be traded through his share-dealing account.
